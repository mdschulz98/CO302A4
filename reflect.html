After The Stories

In the stories, you faced choices about fake emails, fake websites, internet router settings, free wifi, and facebook/general scams. The tips outlined in response to your choices represent a nice basis for basic internet safety. In this webtext I will elaborate on those principles and attempt to demystify computer security, also called cybersecurity. Computer security is a branch of computer science dealing with the safety of information on and traveling between computers. It is both extremely important and extremely hard to implement and understand, and I will to translate the most relevant parts of it into a clear and accessible form. The most important part of computer security for everyone to understand is the importance of using an antivirus, and I will examine and explain this statement. The bottom line of elementary computer security is that everyone should utilize antivirus software and apply basic internet security practices when interacting with computers.

Importance and Difficulty

Cybersecurity evolves and changes as we grow to understand both computers and security better. Additionally and unfortunately, there are two sides to to every coin: for every security-minded lawful programmer there are many hackers trying to steal your data. Combining the relative novelty of this topic, the aforementioned ratio, and the difficulty of securing online entities, I present it is not a stretch and may even be an understatment to call cybersecurity extremely difficult. One of the reasons cybersecurity is so tough is demonstrated by Wallden and Kashefi in their article "Cyber Security in the Quantum Era", where the implications of quantum computing on current security meaures is discussed and analyzed. They find that in quantum computing the field is going to have to advance further to handle computing that is not currently possible. For instance, RSA is an encryption mechnanism used by social media sites, banks, and more to make sure your data isn't readable to anyone but you, and "it would be the demise of RSA should quantum computing happen" (Numberphile2). However, Wallden and Kashefi also argue that quantum methods for security can and will be stronger than RSA and other current algorithms. There are many conflicting views of the effect quantum computing will have on the world of computer security, and that just adds to the difficulty of understanding the topic.

Internet access and usage in the modern age is nearly ubiquitous and required; it is hard to find many remaining occupations or hobbies that don't involve the internet in some way. This means there's a lot of internet traffic and users, and the hackers mentioned above learned pretty quickly that the internet isn't safe on its own. Thus, computer security is of utmost importance to make sure the millions and billions of internet users are keeping their information secure. Johnson, Mohr, Boyd, and Whittaker each tell stories of when information hasn't been secure - ranging from backdoors in Linux distributions to how hackers can steal your credentials at your local Starbucks. The gist of each story is the same: internet users were not applying safe practices and it opened up vulnerabilties that could've (or did) lead to data exposure.

Whittaker tells of a hacker who goes by "Peace" subverting Linux Mint (an operating system, like Windows) downloads to add a backdoor and create a botnet with those who downloaded the subverted version. Unlike the other stories, this one has the least involvement with regular internet users, as most of them use Windows or Mac OS, but is still very dangerous. The most dangerous hacks and exploits are the ones the user never even knows about, and since the botnet and backdoor would be invisible to any user who wasn't explicitly looking for them, many of the victims of this backdoor would likely never have found out. This is the importance of computer security in a nutshell; it is the responsibility of the computer scientists who actually look for those vulnerablities to help the regular users browse the internet safe. However, and more importantly, it is the responsibility of the regular users to trust those computer scientists and employ the strategies they lay out for them. That all sounds well and good, but what are those strategies?

Research

This section contains the various basic internet safety practices compiled from my research; this is by no means a comprehensive list. However, following the practices laid out here will likely be enough to secure any normal internet users activity. By normal here I mean those not involved in the corporate or academic world of computer science, eg. liberal arts professors all the way to 14 year olds who just want to play Minecraft. The first few practices are inspired by a list created by Hoffman and Toaz for How-To Geek, a site dedicated to explaining technology. They first strongly recommend enabling automatic updates for your operating system and applications. Since almost every application and software you use has vulnerabilities, whether they've been found yet or not, it is very important you always have the most up-to-date versions with up-to-date security. For instance, when a new attack vector or exploit is discovered, the creators of the software may send out a hotfix within a day, but if you don't automatically update and don't check, your version it may contain the vulnerability for a longer period of time.

Hoffman and Toaz continue to mention using antivirus anti-malware software. But what if you're careful on the internet already, like when you apply all the tips you learned in the stories of this webtext? Do you still need an antivirus? The short answer is yes, and it boils down to some interesting ramifications of malware. Being safe on the internet by analyzing hyperlinks, using strong passwords, and detecting fake websites and scams works very well for protecting yourself from yourself. However, it doesn't work so well at protecting you from new and dangerous exploits found by hackers each and every day, and that's where antivirus comes into play. Antivirus software is getting better and better at being able to adapt to new viruses, and some are even employing machine learning to detect new malware. The bottom line is that safe internet practices will protect you from most threats, but that extra and arguably necessary line of defense is antivirus software. The built-in Windows Defender antivirus should be good enough for most home Windows users, and it won't ask you to install a bunch of other software like many other antiviruses will.

The last remark of Hoffman and Toaz I would like to mention is to never leave your electronics unattended. It sounds extremely simple, and is, but is one strong security practice that is violated very often (Gersch)! Imagine your laptop's data is encrypted, you have a very strong password to unlock it, you have an antivirus, and you always apply safe internet strategies, but you leave it open while you use the restroom. Now I, playing the role of hacker in this scenario, walk up to your laptop, say, turn off your antivirus, send a copy of all your files to myself, download as much malware as I please, then delete any trace I was ever there. Even better, I'll just steal your laptop and do this all from the comfort of my own home. You did everything else right! But, none of it mattered because I was able to access your laptop that you left for me to mess with. Hennig, in her own list of best cybersecurity practices recommends having a "Find My Phone" app to make sure you have a way to delete data if your device gets compromised and/or stolen. Hoffman, Toaz, Hennig and I all strongly recommend not only to lock your devices if you're away from them, but carry them with you wherever you go if at all possible.

To finish out out the best practices I'd like to list here, that operate in conjuction with everything you learned from the questions, I'd like to divert away from the actual usage of the internet and applications slightly. Hennig, who opens her list of practices by advising you to evaluate your security needs, discusses creating backups of all your data. This refers to your files and application data that need securing. The idea is to create backups because the safest procedure after your data is compromised (however it may be compromised) is to delete it then restore it from a backup. In this way, the attacker loses any information they had over you, and you don't lose any data in the end. Truthfully, backups, whether offline (safer) or online (easier), are a good idea regardless of security. Computers are a wild brain-child and conglomeration of electrical engineering and our preposterous success in tricking rocks to think, so things are bound to go wrong sometimes. Computers crash, hardware dies, unexpected things happen. Be safe, both in terms of security and data integrity, by having backups of your data.

Rounding out these tips and strategies is Hennig and the use of a password manager and the large number of benefitsthat come with using one. Not only are most good enough are recognizing login input forms on websites (that you've been to) to automatically enter your stored credentials, but some can even suggest strong passwords for you. The real win is that almost all will encrypt your stored passwords so all you have to remember is one master password to access them. Of course, that means you need to have a really good master password and keep it secure, but that's much easier, and it turns out, much more secure than trying to remember whether you wrote "c4ulif1ow3r" or "Cau1ifl0wEr", etc. for each and every account. Passwords like those, with random capital letters and numbers in place of the correct letters to spell a real world are very easy to guess by computer and brute force. Passwords suggested by password managers tend to have much more entropy, or randomness, and are therefore much harder to brute force guess, ie. just try a bunch of combinations until one works.

Applying the five big practices in this section in addition to everything you learned in the stories from earlier will make the internet a much safer place for you to browse and interact with others. Always remember to check email links, check website URL's and domain names, not connect to free public wifi without a VPN, change your default router settings, and watch out for things that seem too good to be true. Adding automatic updates, antivirus software, keeping your devices on you, making regular data backups, and using a password manager doesn't seem to bad after that. With these 10 things in mind, not only will you keep any cybersecurity-minded people in your life very happy, you'll be protecting your data, money and online image much better than most. Cybercrime is a lot like robbery in this way - the minimum defense will likely deter criminals.

More On Antivirus Software

One of the most important of the 10 best security practices I've outlined is using antivirus and anti-malware software, and I'd like to look at two more reasons endorsing their use. The first comes from a popular tech-related YouTube channel based in Canada called Linus Tech Tips, that also happens to be one of my favorites. In a particular video, "Linus visits the SKETCHIEST sites on the Web", Linus Sebastian, the creator and main figure of Linus Tech Tips does exactly what the title says he does. However, he is using Bitdefender, an antivirus that claims to stop Day 0 attacks, stop ransomeware from encrypting your folders, and totally protect you from malicious websites. Surprisingly, at least to me, Linus is basically unable to corrupt his computer even while visiting a compiled list of the sketchiest websites and clicking every link he sees. Bitdefender succeeds in stopping him from downloading malware and even visiting many of the websites! This just shows how good modern antivirus software has gotten, and even though you likely won't be doing the same thing Linus was, it helps to have an extra strong line of defense against malware and bad websites.

Closing

Through the story and this webtext the basics of computer security and advocacy of antivirus software have been discussed and clarified. The field of computer security has much, much more depth, and so does its counterpart hacking. This means there are innumerably more security topics to be worried about, however, that is the job of cybersecurity professionals and trained, experienced programmers - not the average internet user. Fortuitously, average users can abstract the responsibility of complicated cybersecurity to the programmers, but the responsiblity of using software and the internet safely is immutably the user's. The knowledge of basic cybersecurity principles (review each mention here) and user of and antivirus is ordinarily enough to fulfill that duty. I encourage all internet users to make sure they are aware of and understand basic internet safety practices and to make use of antivirus software.
